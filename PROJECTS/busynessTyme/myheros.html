In Decisive Treatise, Averroes argues that philosophy—which for him represented conclusions reached using reason and careful method—cannot contradict revelations in Islam, because they are just two different methods of reaching the truth, and "truth cannot contradict truth".[62][63] When conclusions reached by philosophy appear to contradict the text of the revelation, according to Averroes must be subjected to interpretation or allegorical understanding in order to remove the contradiction.[62][59] This interpretation must be done by those "rooted in knowledge"—a phrase taken by from the Quran 3:7, which, for Averroes, refer to philosophers who during his time had access to the "highest methods of knowledge".[62][63] He also argues that the Quran calls for Muslims to study philosophy, as the study and reflection of nature would increase a person's knowledge of "the Artisan" (God).[64] He quotes Quranic passages calling on Muslims to reflect on nature, and used them to render a fatwa (legal opinion) that philosophy is not only allowed for Muslims, but even obligatory, at least among those who have the talent.[65] Averroes also distinguishes three modes of discourse: the rhetorical (based on persuasion), accessible to the common masses; dialectical (based on debate), often employed by theologians and the ulama'; and demonstrative (based on logical deduction) which were employed by philosophers.[59][64] According to Averroes, the Quran uses the rhetorical method of inviting people to the truth, which allows it to reach the common masses with its persuasiveness.[66] Philosophy, on the other hand, uses the demonstrative methods which were only available to the learned but provided the best possible understanding and knowledge.[66]
